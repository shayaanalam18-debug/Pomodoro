Shayaan's Pomodoro Project

Introduction

Project Guiding Question

How can I leverage a pomodoro timer that encourages good habits while discouraging bad habits?

What is Pomodoro?

Based on existing research, Pomodoro is the method created by Francesco Cirillo, CEO of XPLabs in Germany, in the late 1980s aiming to reduce cognitive load, and make studying easier. Pomodoro is based on the straightforward structure of 25 minute blocks with a 5 minute break repeated in cycles of 4. As such, it encourages good habits while discouraging bad habits such as increasing focus for longer periods of time and reducing mental fatigue.

It is especially strong for subjects related to intense memorization like anatomy (Ogut 2025). Moreover, it provides structural studying habits for diverse groups of people to effectively resolve many work related problems.

Based on the numerous research papers I have reviewed, the effectiveness of the Pomodoro method can be understood when broken into 4 aspects:

  • Habit formation
  • Procrastination
  • Concentration
  • Motivation

I provide a more detailed explanation of each of these aspects in the Research section.

Reference: Ogut, E. Assessing the efficacy of the Pomodoro technique in enhancing anatomy lesson retention during study sessions: a scoping review. BMC Med Educ 25, 1440 (2025). https://doi.org/10.1186/s12909-025-08001-0

Research Notes

Habit formation

Habit formation is the process of making repeated behaviours automatic based on a signal. In this case, the signal is the timer on the pomodoro website. The Pomodoro does this by starting a timer (signal) then completing the session, and then giving a break. Pomodoro is based on the straightforward structure of 25 minute blocks with a 5 minute break repeated in cycles of 4 (Smits et al., 2025). This structure helps reduce overall cognitive load by breaking tasks into smaller and easier tasks that feel less overwhelming through the use of timers. Thus, the use of short timed sessions increases motivation by setting clear and achievable goals. The use of pomodoro over time encourages consistent routines allowing for difficult tasks to become easier to complete and reduce procrastination.

Reference: Smits, E. J. C., Wenzel, N., & de Bruin, A. (2025). Investigating the effectiveness of self-regulated, Pomodoro, and Flowtime break-taking techniques among students. Behavioral Sciences, 15(7), 861. https://doi.org/10.3390/bs15070861 PMC article

Procrastination

When a task is too overwhelming or too difficult to start then procrastination often happens. The Temporal Motivation Theory suggests that people are more likely to procrastinate when the task is too large, or the reward is delayed (Steel et al., 2018). Using the Pomodoro technique, procrastination can be prevented as the time used when working in short sessions makes tasks easier to start with a feeling of accomplishment (reward). Moreover, people can be motivated by the “good feeling” after frequent breaks.

Reference: Steel, P., Svartdal, F., Thundiyil, T., & Brothen, T. (2018). Examining procrastination across multiple goal stages: A longitudinal study of temporal motivation theory. Frontiers in Psychology, 9, 327. https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5891720/ PMC article

Concentration (Attention residue)

Attention residue is when a person switches from one task to another and some of their attention stays on the previous task. Research conducted by Leroy (2009) has shown that switching between tasks is detrimental. Using a single task for a set time (pomodoro) can be effective for people to reduce their attention residue, and working in set blocks without distraction enables people to concentrate better without losing focus.

Reference: Leroy, S. (2009). Why is it so hard to do my work? The challenge of attention residue when switching between work tasks. Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes, 109(2), 168–181. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.obhdp.2009.04.002

Motivation (Dopamine reward system)

Motivation gets affected by the dopamine reward system in the brain. This is a reward given for an action because of a positive outcome. Research shows that dopamine is released in response to a reward, and the chances of repeating that specific action increase (Schultz, 1998). The Pomodoro technique uses reward in the form of breaks after a set amount of work. Additionally, each session is a reward for being productive, and it motivates people to use it repeatedly.

Reference: Schultz, W. (1998). Predictive reward signal of dopamine neurons. Journal of Neurophysiology. https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6572474/
